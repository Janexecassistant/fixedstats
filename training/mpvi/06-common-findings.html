Common Findings & How to Show Them

A working playbook for the six findings you record videos on most often. For each one: where to point the camera, what to say, what to avoid.

Lesson Objective

Know exactly how to show and explain the top six findings every technician records — so they look the same, sound the same, and convert at the same rate across every tech on the floor.

1. Tires

What to show: Tread depth gauge reading. Wear pattern across the width. Sidewall condition.

Camera position: Phone 8–12 inches from the tire, gauge or finger pressed into the tread. Pan slowly across the width to show inside, center, outside wear.

Word track — uneven wear:

"This is your front driver tire. [gauge in center] Center tread reads 6/32. [move gauge to inside edge] But on the inside edge, we're down to 3/32. That tells us this side is wearing way faster — usually an alignment issue. I'd want to flag that for the advisor."

Avoid: Vague language like "tires are bad." Show the numbers, show the location of the wear, let the customer see the contrast.

2. Brakes

What to show: Pad thickness. Rotor condition. Brake fluid level and color.

Camera position: Phone right at the caliper opening. Aim a flashlight into the gap. Use a small ruler, screwdriver, or your finger as a thickness reference.

Word track — worn pad:

"Your front passenger brake — [light on the caliper, finger pointing at the pad] the silver-looking strip between the metal backing and the rotor is the friction material. That's what stops your car. New, it's about 12 millimeters thick. Yours is down to about 3. That's why we're flagging it for replacement soon."

Avoid: Showing the wheel on instead of off — you can't see the pad clearly. Take the wheel off if you're filming brakes.

3. Battery

What to show: Date sticker on the battery. Voltage test result. Terminal condition. Tester printout if your shop uses one.

Camera position: Show the date sticker first. Then the tester display with the reading. Close-up on terminals if corroded.

Word track — aging battery:

"This is your battery — [finger on date sticker] see this date code? Manufactured five years ago. [show tester] Tester is reading 'replace' — capacity has dropped below what your car needs to start reliably, especially when it gets cold. This is the one I'd recommend doing before you head out of town this winter."

Avoid: Just saying "your battery's bad." Show the test result, the date, and let the customer see the evidence.

4. Fluids

What to show: Color, level, and clarity. For brake fluid, transmission fluid, coolant: put a sample on a clean white rag to show actual color.

Camera position: Phone above the rag with the fluid sample. Bright light on the sample. Compare with a fresh sample if you have one.

Word track — dirty brake fluid:

"This is your brake fluid — [show sample on rag] see the dark, almost black color? Fresh brake fluid is light amber, almost yellow. This has absorbed a lot of moisture over time. Doesn't mean your brakes are failing right now, but a fluid flush keeps the brake system healthy long-term."

Avoid: Filming fluid in the reservoir with bad lighting — customer can't tell what they're looking at. Pull a sample, put it on white, light it.

5. Belts & Hoses

What to show: Cracking, glazing, fraying on belts. Soft spots, swelling, or wetness on hoses.

Camera position: Phone close enough to see surface detail. Move slowly along the length of the belt or hose so the customer can see the full picture.

Word track — cracked belt:

"I want to show you your serpentine belt — [finger running along the belt, light on it] see all the small cracks running across? That's age and wear. A new belt is smooth. This belt is about ready to slip or break, and when it does, you lose power steering and your battery stops charging. This is a maintenance item we'd want to take care of."

Avoid: Filming the belt at speed — too blurry. Engine off, belt still, finger pointing.

6. Air & Cabin Filters

What to show: Old filter next to a clean one for direct comparison. The contrast does the selling.

Camera position: Both filters in frame, well-lit, side by side on a clean surface. Touch each one — the old one collapses a little, the new one is firm.

Word track — dirty cabin filter:

"Want to show you this real quick — [hold both filters] on the left is the new cabin filter for your car. On the right is the one we pulled out today. [shake the dirty one over a rag — debris falls] All that dust, leaves, and pollen — your AC has been blowing through that. Replacement is quick and inexpensive, and your air quality changes immediately."

Avoid: Filming filters with no comparison. The customer doesn't know what "clean" looks like. Always have a new one in the shot.

Manager Coaching Tip

Pick one finding category per week as a focus. "This week we're going to nail brake videos." Watch every tech's brake videos that week, coach to the standard, then move to the next category next week. Eight weeks and the whole field guide is internalized.
