The First 10 Seconds

Customers decide in the first 10 seconds whether to watch the rest of your video. Get the opening right and they stay through the whole thing. Get it wrong and they bail before you've even gotten to the finding.

Lesson Objective

Open every video with the same four-part structure so the customer knows immediately who they're watching, what car they're looking at, and why it matters.

The Four-Part Opening

Every Dyer video opens the same way. Customers should be able to recognize it within two seconds.

  1. Who you are. Name and that you're the technician working on the car.
  2. What car you're looking at. Year, make, model. Confirms to the customer this is their vehicle.
  3. What you found. One sentence — the headline.
  4. What you're about to show them. Bridge into the body of the video.

Word Tracks

Standard opening (one finding):

"Hey Mr. Carter — this is Marcus, your technician here at Dyer. I'm working on your 2019 Tahoe. I want to show you the front tires — they're wearing on the inside edge, and you can see it clearly from this angle. Let me bring you in close."

Standard opening (multiple findings):

"Hey Sarah — this is Marcus, working on your 2021 RAV4 here at Dyer. I went through the full inspection and there's three things I want to walk you through: the rear brakes, your battery, and the cabin filter. Let's start with the brakes — come over to the driver's side with me."

Good news / green video:

"Hi Mr. Patel — this is Marcus, your tech at Dyer. I'm finishing up the inspection on your 2020 Camry. Real quick — everything checks out solid. Tires are in great shape, brakes have plenty of life, fluids look clean. I just wanted to show you a couple things so you can see what we're seeing. Let me walk you through it."

Customer concern follow-up:

"Hi Ms. Williams — this is Marcus at Dyer, working on your Acura. You'd mentioned a noise when braking — I got the wheels off and I want to show you exactly what's causing it. Take a look at this rotor."

What NOT to Say in the Opening

"Okay so... yeah... uh, this is the car..."

You're already off. Customer is wondering if you know what you're doing.

"So I found a BUNCH of stuff on this car..."

"A bunch" makes the customer defensive. They hear "I'm about to be sold a lot of work." Stick to specifics — three findings, max.

"Hey, your service advisor wanted me to show you this..."

Doesn't introduce you. Doesn't acknowledge the car is theirs. Sounds like a forced exercise. Take ownership — you found it, you're showing it.

"Alright so... let me see... is this the Camry? Yeah, this is the Camry..."

You're checking your own work on camera. Should have been confirmed before recording started.

Tone & Energy

The opening is also where your tone is set. Customers form an impression of you in the first sentence.

Aim forAvoid
Calm, professional, warmRobotic monotone or scripted-sounding
Confident — like you know your craftHesitant, mumbled, unsure
Conversational, like you're talking to one personSalesy, urgent, hard-pressure
Direct, no filler words"Um, uh, like, basically, kind of"

One Last Thing: Smile

You can hear a smile on a video, even when the camera is pointed at a brake rotor. Customers respond to the energy in your voice. You don't need to be a personality — just sound like someone who's glad to be doing their job. That tone alone closes more work than any sales script.

Manager Coaching Tip

Listen only to the first 10 seconds of five random videos from each tech this week. Score: did they (1) state their name, (2) state the vehicle, (3) state the finding, (4) bridge into the body? If they missed any of those, coach the four-part opening specifically. It's the easiest part of the video to get consistent.
